Isaac Newton was born on December 25, 1642, in Woolsthorpe-by-Colsterworth, a small village in Lincolnshire, England. He became one of the most influential scientists in human history, making groundbreaking contributions to mathematics, physics, and astronomy. Newton formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, which laid the foundation for classical mechanics and dominated scientific understanding for over two centuries. His work "Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica," published in 1687, is considered one of the most important scientific works ever written. Newton also made seminal contributions to optics, demonstrating that white light is composed of a spectrum of colors using a prism. He independently developed calculus, a mathematical framework essential to modern science and engineering. Newton served as Warden and later Master of the Royal Mint, and was elected President of the Royal Society in 1703. He was knighted by Queen Anne in 1705. Newton died in 1727 in London, leaving behind a legacy that fundamentally shaped modern science.
