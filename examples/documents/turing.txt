Alan Turing was born on June 23, 1912, in Maida Vale, London, England. He was a mathematician, computer scientist, logician, and cryptanalyst who is widely considered the father of theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence. In 1936, Turing published his seminal paper introducing the concept of the Turing machine, an abstract computational model that formalized the concepts of algorithm and computation. During World War II, Turing played a crucial role at Bletchley Park, where he led efforts to break the German Enigma cipher. His work in cryptanalysis is estimated to have shortened the war by several years and saved countless lives. After the war, Turing worked on the design of the Automatic Computing Engine, one of the first designs for a stored-program computer. He also proposed the Turing test as a measure of machine intelligence. Turing made important contributions to mathematical biology, particularly in morphogenesis. Tragically, he was prosecuted for homosexuality in 1952 and died on June 7, 1954. He received a posthumous royal pardon in 2013.
