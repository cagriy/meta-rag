Marie Curie was born Maria Sklodowska on November 7, 1867, in Warsaw, Poland, which was then part of the Russian Empire. She was a pioneering physicist and chemist who conducted groundbreaking research on radioactivity, a term she herself coined. Curie was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize and remains the only person to have won Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields: Physics in 1903 and Chemistry in 1911. Together with her husband Pierre Curie, she discovered two new elements, polonium and radium. Her meticulous research into radioactive substances laid the foundation for modern nuclear physics and cancer treatment through radiation therapy. During World War I, Curie developed mobile radiography units, known as "petites Curies," to provide X-ray services to field hospitals. She became the first female professor at the University of Paris in 1906. Curie died on July 4, 1934, from aplastic anemia, likely caused by prolonged exposure to radiation during her research.
