Emmy Noether was born Amalie Emmy Noether on March 23, 1882, in Erlangen, Bavaria, Germany. She was a mathematician who made fundamental contributions to abstract algebra and theoretical physics. Noether's theorem, published in 1918, establishes a profound connection between symmetries in physics and conservation laws, and is considered one of the most important mathematical theorems in modern physics. Her work in abstract algebra revolutionized the field, particularly through her contributions to ring theory, group representations, and the theory of ideals. Despite facing significant gender discrimination throughout her career, Noether became one of the most influential mathematicians of the twentieth century. She was denied a regular faculty position for years at the University of Gottingen, often lecturing under a male colleague's name. Albert Einstein described her as the most important woman in the history of mathematics. Forced to flee Nazi Germany in 1933, she emigrated to the United States and joined the faculty at Bryn Mawr College. Noether died on April 14, 1935, from complications following surgery.
