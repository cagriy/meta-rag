Albert Einstein was born on March 14, 1879, in Ulm, in the Kingdom of Wurttemberg in the German Empire. He is widely regarded as one of the greatest theoretical physicists of all time. In 1905, often called his "miracle year," Einstein published four revolutionary papers that transformed modern physics. These included his special theory of relativity, the photoelectric effect, Brownian motion, and mass-energy equivalence expressed by the famous equation E=mc squared. His general theory of relativity, published in 1915, redefined the understanding of gravity as a curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy. Einstein received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 for his explanation of the photoelectric effect, which was pivotal in the development of quantum theory. He emigrated to the United States in 1933, fleeing the rise of Nazism, and joined the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey. Einstein became an American citizen in 1940 and continued his scientific work until his death on April 18, 1955.
