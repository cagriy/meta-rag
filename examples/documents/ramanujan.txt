Srinivasa Ramanujan was born on December 22, 1887, in Erode, a small town in the Madras Presidency of British India. He grew up in Kumbakonam, where his family lived in modest circumstances. His father worked as a clerk in a cloth merchant's shop, and his mother was a homemaker who sang devotional songs at a local temple. Ramanujan showed extraordinary mathematical talent from a very young age. By the time he was eleven, he had exhausted the mathematical knowledge of two college students who lodged at his home. At thirteen, he discovered and mastered an advanced textbook on trigonometry. By fifteen, he had obtained a copy of George Shoobridge Carr's "Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics," a dense compendium of thousands of mathematical theorems presented without proofs. This book became his primary self-study resource, and he worked through its results independently, developing his own methods and notations. Ramanujan's obsessive devotion to mathematics came at a cost to his formal education. He repeatedly failed his college examinations because he neglected all subjects other than mathematics, losing his scholarship and eventually dropping out entirely.

Despite lacking formal academic credentials, Ramanujan continued to produce an astonishing volume of original mathematical work in isolation. He filled notebooks with theorems on infinite series, continued fractions, number theory, and modular equations, often arriving at results through an intuitive process that he could not always explain formally. In 1912, he secured a modest clerical position at the Madras Port Trust, where his supervisor, S. Narayana Iyer, recognized his mathematical gifts and encouraged him to contact established mathematicians abroad. After several unsuccessful attempts to gain recognition from British academics, Ramanujan wrote a now-famous letter to G. H. Hardy at the University of Cambridge in January 1913. The letter contained over one hundred mathematical results with almost no proofs. Hardy, initially skeptical, examined the results with his colleague J. E. Littlewood and concluded that they could "only be written by a mathematician of the highest class." Hardy arranged for Ramanujan to travel to Cambridge, where he arrived in April 1914.

Ramanujan's five years at Cambridge were extraordinarily productive and represent the pinnacle of his mathematical career. Working with Hardy, he published numerous papers on topics including highly composite numbers, partition functions, and the properties of the Riemann zeta function. Their collaboration produced the famous Hardy-Ramanujan asymptotic formula for the number of partitions of an integer, a result of remarkable depth and elegance. Ramanujan was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1918, one of the youngest to receive this honor, and was also elected a Fellow of Trinity College, Cambridge, the first Indian to achieve this distinction. However, Ramanujan struggled deeply during his time in England. The cold climate, the difficulty of maintaining his strict vegetarian diet during wartime rationing, and his isolation from family and cultural community took a severe toll on his health. He was diagnosed with tuberculosis and a severe vitamin deficiency, and he spent long periods in sanatoriums. In 1919, his health deteriorated to the point that he returned to India, hoping that the familiar climate and food would aid his recovery. Tragically, Ramanujan died on April 26, 1920, at the age of just thirty-two, in Kumbakonam. After his death, his notebooks continued to inspire generations of mathematicians, and the so-called "lost notebook" discovered in 1976 contained hundreds of previously unknown results that are still being studied and proved today.
