Charles Darwin was born on February 12, 1809, in Shrewsbury, Shropshire, England. He was a naturalist and biologist best known for his contributions to the science of evolution. His proposition that all species of life descended from common ancestors through the process of natural selection is now widely accepted as a fundamental concept in science. Darwin's five-year voyage on HMS Beagle, beginning in 1831, took him around the world and provided the observations that formed the basis of his evolutionary theory. His landmark work "On the Origin of Species," published in 1859, presented overwhelming evidence for evolution and sparked intense scientific and public debate. Darwin also studied the formation of coral reefs, the geology of volcanic islands, and the expression of emotions in humans and animals. His later work "The Descent of Man" applied evolutionary theory to human origins. Darwin was a Fellow of the Royal Society and received the Copley Medal in 1864. He died on April 19, 1882, and was honored with a state funeral at Westminster Abbey.
