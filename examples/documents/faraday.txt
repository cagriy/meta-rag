Michael Faraday was born on September 22, 1791, in Newington Butts, a suburb of London, England. He came from a poor family and received only a basic formal education. At the age of fourteen, he became an apprentice to a local bookbinder named George Riebau, where he developed a deep fascination with science by reading the books that came through the shop. One book in particular, "Conversations on Chemistry" by Jane Marcet, ignited his lifelong passion for experimental science. Faraday attended lectures by the renowned chemist Humphry Davy at the Royal Institution in 1812 and took meticulous notes, which he bound into a book and sent to Davy as a demonstration of his dedication. Impressed, Davy hired Faraday as his laboratory assistant in 1813. Over the following years, Faraday accompanied Davy on a grand tour of Europe, meeting many of the continent's leading scientists and gaining invaluable exposure to the broader scientific community. Faraday's early work at the Royal Institution focused on chemistry, where he made several important contributions including the discovery of benzene in 1825 and the liquefaction of chlorine.

Faraday's most groundbreaking work, however, came in the field of electromagnetism. In 1831, he discovered electromagnetic induction, demonstrating that a changing magnetic field could produce an electric current in a nearby conductor. This discovery laid the foundation for the development of electric generators and transformers, technologies that would eventually power the modern world. Faraday also formulated the laws of electrolysis, establishing the quantitative relationship between electricity and chemical change. His concept of "lines of force" to describe magnetic and electric fields was initially dismissed by the mathematical physicists of his day, but it later became the basis for James Clerk Maxwell's electromagnetic field theory. Despite having no formal mathematical training, Faraday's physical intuition and experimental brilliance allowed him to make discoveries that fundamentally reshaped the understanding of electricity, magnetism, and their deep interconnection.

Throughout his career, Faraday remained at the Royal Institution, eventually becoming its director and Fullerian Professor of Chemistry. He was offered a knighthood and the presidency of the Royal Society on multiple occasions, but he declined all such honors, preferring to remain simply "Mr. Faraday." He was deeply religious, a devoted member of the Sandemanian church, and his faith played an important role in his personal life. Faraday gave a famous series of Christmas Lectures at the Royal Institution, including "The Chemical History of a Candle," which became one of the most celebrated popular science presentations in history. He suffered from memory loss and cognitive decline in his later years, likely caused by chronic exposure to mercury and other chemicals in his laboratory. Faraday retired from active research in 1858 and was given a grace-and-favour house at Hampton Court by Queen Victoria. He died on August 25, 1867, at the age of seventy-five, leaving behind a legacy that transformed both science and technology.
